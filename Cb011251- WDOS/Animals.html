Animals in Sri Lanka

Sri Lanka is known for its rich biodiversity, and the country is home to a diverse array of animals, many of which
are endemic to the region. Here are some notable facts about animals in Sri Lanka. Elephants: Sri Lanka has a
significant population of Asian elephants (Elephas maximus). These elephants can be found in various national parks,
and some, like the Elephas maximus maximus subspecies, are native to the island. Leopards: The Sri Lankan leopard
(Panthera pardus kotiya) is a subspecies of leopard that is endemic to the island. Yala National Park is known for its
leopard population. Birds: Sri Lanka is a birdwatcher's paradise, boasting a high number of bird species, including
endemics like the Sri Lanka Junglefowl and the Sri Lanka Blue Magpie.

Bundala National Park and Sinharaja Forest Reserve are popular birdwatching destinations. Marine Life: The waters
surrounding Sri Lanka are rich in marine life. The island is a hotspot for whale watching, and you can find various
species of dolphins, whales, and sea turtles off the coasts. Deer: Spotted deer, sambar deer, and barking deer are
among the deer species found in Sri Lanka. These animals are often spotted in national parks. Sloth Bears: The sloth
bear (Melursus ursinus inornatus) is another notable species found in Sri Lanka. Wilpattu National Park is one of the
areas where sloth bears can be encountered. Reptiles: Sri Lanka is home to a variety of reptiles, including crocodiles,
monitor lizards, and a diverse range of snakes. The island has a mix of venomous and non-venomous snake species.
Endemic Amphibians: Sri Lanka has numerous endemic amphibians, particularly in the wet zone. The island's montane
regions harbor unique species of frogs and toads. Conservation efforts in Sri Lanka are ongoing to protect and preserve
its diverse wildlife.

YALA NATIONAL PARK

Yala combines a strict nature reserve with a national park. Divided into 5 blocks, the park has a protected area
of nearly 130,000 hectares of land consisting of light forests, scrubs, grasslands, tanks and lagoons. Two blocks
are currently opened to the public.Situated in Sri Lanka's south-east hugging the panoramic Indian Ocean, Yala
was designated a wildlife sanctuary in 1900 and was designated a national park in 1938. Ironically, the park was
initially used as a hunting ground for the elite under British rule. Yala is home to 44 varieties of mammal and 215
bird species. Among its more famous residents are the world's biggest concentration of leopards, majestic elephants,
sloth bears, sambars, jackals, spotted dear, peacocks, and crocodiles. The best time to visit Yala is between February
and July when the water levels of the park are quite low, bringing animals into the open.

Yala has one of the highest leopard densities in the world. The Sri Lankan leopard is the star attraction,
and visitors often have a chance to spot these elusive big cats, particularly in the Block I region of the park.
The park features diverse habitats, including grasslands, scrublands, forests, and coastal areas. This diversity
contributes to the wide array of wildlife species that inhabit the park. Yala has several natural lakes and tanks,
providing essential water sources for wildlife. These water bodies attract a variety of animals, especially during
the dry season.
